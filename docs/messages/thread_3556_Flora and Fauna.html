Flora and Fauna

From: donutz716 | Date: 2007-07-11 21:21:33 UTC
Would you purposely step on a lady slipper? Do you respect the flora and fauna while hiking? It was my sad discovery today that a stone wall was ripped apart in several sections while someone was searching for my box. Please leave our woods the same way or better when hiking for letterboxes. If you are looking for a box in a stone wall, move 1 or 2 rocks and if it's not there, then put the rocks back. The stone walls were made by our ancestors. Please respect them. They are part of our history. Wouldn't it be nice if generations in the future can admire them just as we do now? Not to mention that Park Services and people in charge of Preserves may one day decide that there should be no letterboxing in those areas due to negligent boxers. Please - respect our woods.

Enjoy!
donutz716

Re: [LbNE] Flora and Fauna

From: Pamela Smith | Date: 2007-07-11 21:59:23 UTC
I think this is why some codes of conduct suggest not planting in a
stone wall in the first place. Overzealous or unthinking LBers will
just tear the wall apart - just as yes, some people will step on the
ladyslipper and no, not everyone respects the flora and fauna.
Planting in any sensitive location puts that location at risk. In
this case, there are people who have no clue about the likely history
of those walls and then there are those who know and don't actually
care, and you're leading them right to it (and telling them their
goal is inside, not near the wall....) along with the responsible
folks. It's just something we all have to think about when we choose
a location to plant.

That said...... DON'T TEAR DOWN STONE WALLS!!!! They're an
interesting part of our nation's history and shouldn't be messed
with, even for a letterbox.

I would love to think we can all plant wherever we'd like without
repercussions, but it doesn't work that way.

pezpam

From: donutz716 | Date: 2007-07-13 17:22:16 UTC
No more boxes in stonewalls for me. My clues are correct, but the issue was that I put paces and they were counting steps. Many people still don't have that straight in the their heads. A pace is 2 foot falls. (left and right or right and left) A step is each time you put your foot on the ground. Also, the person who hid the box last, stuck it way in and up on a rock in the cave so it wasn't easy to spot. Thanks for everyone's comments. I learned a thing or 2 and hope anyone reading might have learned something too. It is a pleasure being part of a group who have a positive approach to an issue.

Enjoy!

I wholeheartedly agree -- not just about stone walls, but about flora
in general. I'm always mildy dismayed when hunting for boxes to
discover their exact location without much effort because of a
noticable "letterboxer's trail" to the box. We are all familiar with
the SPORs and SPOTs -- and many you can probably see these
little "paths" that lead off trail as well. Sometimes they are as
obvious as crushed ferns or other plant life, but often they are as
seemingly benign as the lack of flora where it should likely be.

The challenge is that, perhaps suprisingly, it takes very little to
destroy ground vegetation in the woods. There is a distinct reason
why most conservation and park agencies demand people to stay on
trail. While in order to participate in this hobby, I'm willing
to "bend" that rule a little to forage off trail slightly, I implore
planters -- and finders -- to consider and be cautious of the
potential damage you may due to the plants along the way. Most
woodland groundcover is not like the grass on your front lawn -- it
cannot sustain multiple people trampling it. The result is the lack
of vegegation in a certain area -- the start of a new trail, in
essence -- as recognizable to me as our wonderful SPORs are. As with
SPORs, most non-letterboxers won't notice, but those of us with now-
trained eyes do, and we recognize that what we are noticing is
nothing less than destructive.

I have boxes located in stone walls -- and in tree hollows -- and in
other various natural crooks and caverns. My hope, nay, my
insistence, is that hunters of my boxes do their best to ensure that
they follow the "Leave No Trace" principle to the fullest as
(hopefully) I have tried to do as well.

Best,
CPAScott
