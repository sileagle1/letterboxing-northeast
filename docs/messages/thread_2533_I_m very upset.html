From: mindizney | Date: 2005-11-03 16:45:09 UTC
I do not know the specifics on what happened to spur Koyote to pull his
clues from LBNA, but I do know that some sort of article about
letterboxing was in the local newspaper. I do not know what was in the
article, but I think this brings back an important point.

THE MORE PEOPLE WHO KNOW ABOUT LETTERBOXING, THE INCREASED RISK OUR
BOXES TAKE!

I feel that we should keep letterboxing private. Just a couple of
years ago, I too found out about letterboxing through the news
(Hartford Courant to be precise). However, we have had increasing
traffic these last few years and more boxes have become prey to vandals.

We need to make this effort to keep the hobby private. We can not rely
on the news media to write an appropriate story on letterboxing. I
have seen too many stories where the placements of letterboxes has been
compromised.

I beg all of you. Please keep letterboxing private!

Music Woman in CT
who's saddened by Koyote's decision

From: john@johnsblog.com | Date: 2005-11-03 21:57:13 UTC
I respectfully disagree. I think that the best way to
protect your boxes is to make them slightly challenging or
word of mouth. By slightly challenging, I'm thinking of
requiring the use of a compass or making the hike longer
that .5 miles. Even making your letterbox clues a simple
mystery improves your boxes life expectancy by putting the
clues where newbies are unlikely to look.

New people are coming to letterboxing every day with or
without media help... it just briefly accelerates the
process.

Another approach is education. Maybe we can improve the
"Getting Started" section of letterboxing.org so that it
starts by focusing on the importance of discretion and
rehiding along with the basics of how to stamp in and what
to do when you find a hitchhiker. I love Erik Davis'
article on making a letterbox that's in our Getting
Started section now but I think we need to preface the
section section with what we what newbies to know and what
we expect in terms of etiquette. I think many letterboxes
could be spared a grizzly death with a little better
education. Now we just need a volunteer or three!

Choi

From: Lisa | Date: 2005-11-04 01:07:09 UTC
I understand what you are saying, believe me many of my boxes have been trashed this
past summer, but like the person who just built a new house in the woods and wants a
ban on new development, it really is not possible nor fair to stop spreading the word
about letterboxing. I agree, face to face contact, telling friends about letterboxing is ideal,
but how many of us would be here were it not for SMithsonian's article, for NPR's piece, or
for more recent publicity?

I think the problem lies in part because of letterboxing's appeal being the secrecy (at least
for me)...it is so amazing that this is going on and that most people are oblivious! Its draw
is its downfall. We are at a point in the US where we really cannot stop the media from
spreading the word. But I think there are other things you can do, as a placer: write more
difficult clues. I hate to say it, but most people will not continue to pursue letterboxing if it
is hard and won't grant them instant satisfaction (remember the "stupid and lazy" post
from Sangfroid several months ago? It is true!) You can also pull your clues and distribute
them in other ways. (WOM, alternate websites, inside other boxes etc) I personally find
boxes that are hard to find clues for, hard to figure out and hard to actually find
extremely satisfying.

Also, anyone can trash your boxes, animals, kids, partying teens...not just oblivious
newbies, some of whom may end up being fabulous carvers and placers if given the
correct tools and guidance.

From: Cyndie | Date: 2005-11-04 17:15:10 UTC
I sympathize with the boxer whose name was used in the article
without her permission. I urge you to contact the editor of the
paper. My daughter's full name was used by a journalist in a story
about students with social anxiety. Imagine how THAT helped her
condition!!!

But as for those who want to keep letterboxing private.... I am
amused by this sentiment from many experienced boxers, most who admit
they didn't think up this hobby themselves but learned of it from
reading a magazine or newspaper article. They have the opinion that
now that THEY have learned of it..it's time to shut it down to
others. How ungenerous of them.

I learned of this hobby from the Smithsonian article which my mother
sent to me. By the time I started, she had passed away from cancer so
I was never able to box with her. But she would be proud that I have
made over 100 boxes accessible to all people, while retaining a few
special boxes as WOM. I do not offer myself for interviews but have
taken many newbies under my wing and escorted them to boxes..as well
as taught box etiquette. Just like experienced boxers did for ME.
Yes, some of my boxes have gone missing...mostly due to my own poor
placing skills...and mostly when I was a newbie.

I am forever grateful to those who generously helped me learn and
shared their expertise with me. Mark and Sue Pepe, Catbead, Alafair,
Turtle McQ and a few others, to be exact. They have gained another
person who wants to share the great outdoors, cool places and neat
stamps with others. Do those "PRIVATE ONLY" boxers act this way with
other things? Do they not share a great new restaurant with others..a
good fishing hole...a store running a great sale..where to get lock-
n'lock?

My OWN opinion..is if you want to make ALL your boxes WOM or for a
select few...do it. I won't miss your boxes in the least. But I urge
you to try EDUCATING new boxers...not EXCLUDING them. You were a new
boxer once, weren't you?

Lock Wench

From: Pam | Date: 2005-11-05 03:00:35 UTC
It's not that I want to keep it private, but that the boxes outlined in the article (without the permission of the owners of the letterboxes), could be compromised by people who do not accept the hobby.

It will be interested to see how long the Dunkin Donuts and McDonalds boxes will last now that the clues were plastered into the news article for all to see. The other three boxes whose clues were in the article were inside stores, so they have a better chance of surviving.

Remember, that there are a lot of people out there who do not accept our hobby. For example, the NPS has outlawed letterboxing on National Park land (though they have not outlawed geocaching... which I don't understand). The NPS has been known to confiscate boxes on their land. This could happen anywhere.

I feel as though our boxes that we plant are in many ways, "the box owner's property." Shouldn't the news media respect the owner's wishes before putting information in a news article?

I believe there was an instance in the southeastern part of the US within the last year that the reporter of a news story actually put the entire clue in a story. (do I have that right, or is my memory failing me?) These acts of reporters greatly compromise the boxes in question.

I've seen too many boxes, which were in very good hiding places, go missing, stamps stolen, nasty things written inside the boxes etc... (many were my own boxes) mostly due to non-letterboxer interaction.

And yes, I do have two messages in at the newspaper for the reporter.

From: nemonh72 | Date: 2005-11-18 11:57:56 UTC
Make it private?! I hope not. My wife and I recently discovered
letterboxing via an article in a newspaper. We now have a new
favorite hobby.

We were all ready hikers but would often not go because there was
just not enough "draw". A big draw in fact. We just replaced our
old car with a new one, mainly so that we could count on it to make
it all over the place reliably so that we could letter box (we might
be addicts).

So, we are very glad that this article was written. We now have
something to do that doesn't cost much and is extremly rewarding and
enjoyable for us. As I write this we are F9's.

I know you are worried about people trashing your boxes, but secrecy
will not necesarily aid in saving your boxes. Some of the boxes
we've found were almost visible...a bit a white or blue showing in
the rocks and looking out of place from the trail...covering debris
looking very out of place in the surrounding landscape. These could
have been seen, discovered and maybe trashed by the "uneducated"
(and we made an effort to solve this problem with extra leaves and
such). However, they could have lead to new letterboxers who asked
the question upon finding them, "what is this letterboxing thing?"
Some missing boxes might be "missing" because someone who found them
took it upon themselves to take them home and replace wet items. Of
the nine we've found so far, at least 3 were pretty moist and could
probably have used some attention from the planter...so e-mail
contact info is a good thing.

Here's an analogy for you to ponder...
My wife and I work at a store. The store is publicly known of.
People come to the store and most of them use if for what it is
inteded...a place to buy things they want. Others, however, and a
small percentage at that, do cause problems, make messes, steal and
in general disturb our lives. Its like this with anything. These
people are people after all, so what did you expect? Life goes
on...don't let them ruin it for you/us...replant!

Thanks for reading this...and thanks for planting boxes. Hopefully,
we'll start our own plantings in the spring.

Happy LB'n,
NeMo & BB (F9)

From: dave & diane | Date: 2005-11-25 02:20:37 UTC
Here, here!

I've said this before: When I put out a letterbox, I know that it is in peril. That's the chance I take - like asking a girl out on a date, remember? If you go 'secret', then you can still be trashed. The best defenses against being trashed are:
1. DON'T pick a party spot!
2. Letterboxers that take more care in re-hiding than just throwing some leaves over the box.
3. Good initial placement off of the beaten path (more than 2 feet!), but in a place that's not suspicious to be seen in.

My brother-in-law found a letterbox by accident and asked my wife to look up what it all means. She did and now we have a great new hobby. Accidents happen, and can be good.

I, too, have lost letterboxes. It really bites when the box is several states away, too! Will I pull my boxes away from my fellows? Heck, no. Others have, and we are all diminished thereby. And I don't believe that they are more secure now, just less enjoyed.
