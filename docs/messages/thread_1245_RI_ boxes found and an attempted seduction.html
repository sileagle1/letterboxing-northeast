RI: boxes found and an attempted seduction

From: Christopher Martin | Date: 2004-09-07 13:15:12 UTC
Rustypuff and I found the following boxes in place, intact, and dry on
Sunday:

Legendary Creatures #3: The Wendigo
Breaking Hearts at Breakheart
Legendary Creatures #1: Loch Ness Monster
Legendary Creatures #2: Nokkon Wud
Mercy Brown: Exeter Vampire

We also attempted to find First Fallen Heroes, but we're not sure we
were even starting in the right spot, and we had no luck.

Thanks are due to the folks who helped us out with Breaking Hearts, as
well as the folks who helped *them* out with it.

We'd like to note that the original log for Mercy Brown is full, but
that another thoughtful boxer has put in a new one. So there are now
two logs in the box. Bob the Wiz, if you're out there, this is FYI.

Now I'll let Rustypuff tell the story of our attempted seduction:

Hi all!

This is one for the books...

I have been a Naturalist and Environmental Educator for over 15 years,
and have never had quite such an amazing wildlife interaction before
(and I've had quite a few!). This one gave me goosebumps.

We were sitting in a magical spot, stamping in to the Nokkon Wud box.
We were just commenting on how lovely a spot it was and how it was
such a perfect spot for this monster to live. The stream trickled
gently by. Water striders battled in a pool at our feet. A tiny frog
leaped after them. There was a rustling nearby, but I think we just
assumed it was a squirrel. The rustling continued as we began to pack
up the log and stamp in their respective baggies.

Finally we stopped to listen to the rustling as it got closer and
louder and seemed to have another, quieter noise chiming in between
the rustling. It was a peeping and cooing and chortling. Before it
really registered what it might be, we were startled by a movement
behind us. A ruffed grouse had hopped up onto a log. 'Cool!' We
thought and sat very still and watched so as to not scare it away.

For a few minutes we thought that it was just foraging and hadn't
noticed us sitting there, even though it was only about 10 feet away,
but as we began to get cramped up from sitting frozen still and had to
move our legs and necks we realized that it not only knew we were
there, but was checking us out. It didn't care that we moved or spoke
to each other or even snapped the lid of the box back on.

At first I thought it was a female trying to draw our attention away
from youngsters nearby, but since there was no 'mock' broken wing or
body dragging behavior and it is really rather late in the season for
baby grouse, I figured it was a male and that we had happened to sit
right next to his lekking log (grouse, like turkey, stake out a
prominent piece of real estate - called a lek - on which to display
for females. Grouse drum their chests a la Tarzan in the mating season).

Back and forth he wandered, coming ever closer and making cooing and
chucking noises. We realized that we didn't have any need of sitting
still or being quiet. This little one was not only not concerned, he
was displaying for us. I just thought this was so very cool and so
very strange.

The little grouse would stop and cock his head at us, coo, ruffle his
tail and his neck ruff and pick up a leaf and turn around and toss the
leaf so that it would slide down his back. Leaf after leaf, he would
slide them down his back. He came closer and closer.

It got to the point that he was only about a foot away. I wouldn't
have been surprised if he had hopped into CSCM's lap. CSCM picked up a
leaf in response to him and tossed it gently into the air. This seemed
to elicit more cooing and chortling, so he tossed another leaf. Back
and forth they took turns tossing leaves. CSCM reached out his hand
toward the grouse... and got bitten, which I could have told him would
happen. So he returned to tossing leaves. The grouse was very enamored
with us.

This went on for about 20 minutes or so. We finally decided that we
had to get going. The afternoon was wearing on, and we had other plans
for 'boxing that day. We got up, replaced the box, said 'goodbye' to
the grouse and began heading back down the trail. We had only gone a
short way when we heard a frantic chuttering behind us. We turned
around to see the grouse running down the trail after us. When we
stopped, he caught up with us and resumed his little show. We talked
with him a bit more, assuring him that we really wouldn't be a good
match and apologized to him for having to leave. We felt bad as we
walked down the trail and a bit concerned that he might try the same
thing with a predator. He followed us a short way down the trail, but
eventually must have hit the distant edge of his territory and watched
us leave.

We had a great rest of the day, but couldn't help feeling like we had
just left our true love at the train station.
~sniff~

CSCM, f563
Rustypuff, f474
