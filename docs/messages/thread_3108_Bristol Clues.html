Clues
Planted by Rubaduc 4-29-02
Re-planted 11-16-03

Easy walk:
This box is planted (at his favorite park) for my 5 year old (now
7)letterboxing
grandson, Jared, who is stronger, faster and more energetic than any
raptor I ever met.

Directions to Rockwell Park: In Bristol, on Rt. 72 west go 0.6 mi. past
the Rt. 69 intersection, turning right onto Dutton Ave. park in front of
the playground.

The usual routine of playing on the swings etc. comes first and then
follow the sidewalk north through the white gate and over the bridge,
turning left onto the fitness circuit. The large stone building straight
ahead of you when crossing the bridge once housed changing rooms and
concession stand when this was a swimming spot. Once on the gravel
circuit you'll see a good stamping in table on your left and a smaller
stone building ahead of you. Head towards this smaller building, and follow
the arrow on the tree up a few stairs. From the top stair count approximately
65 steps to a three foot high stump with an uprooted tree just beyond it. Jared
now hides in the roots of the tree under a piece of cement. Please be careful
not to be seen by walkers or by cars passing by on the road just above you.
This is Jareds last chance not to become extinct.
Thank you

Page Park Ski Tow
by Music Woman
Find Page Park on Route 229 across from Bristol Eastern High School. Park in
the first lot on the left. Walk past the basketball court and go up the hill.
You will notice that this wide path used to be the ski slope. The poles are
still visible with the wheels for the ski tow still attached. Once at the top of
the hill, at the last pole, go over the paved road onto a path. Go through
the first 4-way intersection and at the second (almost immediately after the
first) go left. Walk 15 paces up the trail and bushwack right 4 paces to small
pieces of ledge. Your prize is hidden between two small pieces of ledge under
sticks.

pretty easy..maybe an hour

This box is planted as a tribute to my summer letterboxing companion, my
grandson Will.
Thank you, Will for all the good times.

Sweet William (according to Webster) is a biennial plant having bracteate
heads of brightly colored flowers.

Directions: From Rt.6 in Bristol turn East onto Hill St. go 0.8 mi. to James
P. Casey Rd. and go right. After 0.6 mi. you'll see the Chippens Hill Middle
School on your left and shortly on your right, a parking area.

Clues: From your car walk down the paved road till you come to a path on the
right, follow this through an open meadow, watching the hill in front of you.
There are several trails leading up the hill but you want the one furthest to
the left (you will have to cut across the grass to achieve this). At the top,
at the tree line there's a white blazed trail going both ways, follow it to
your right. This area is known as "The Hoppers" because of the "kettle holes"
left 12,000 years ago during the ice age. As the ice receded over the gravel
ground, it left huge depressions called hoppers, which you can see on both sides
of the trail as you start walking into the woods. Staying on the white trail
the whole way you'll soon see a blue trail enter and join the white for awhile.
Just before going down a sandy slope you'll see tin discs on the trees to
mark the trail. Blue goes left and white stays straight. Take the white. Some of
this area has been devastated by motorized vehicles, but used to be beautiful
hiking trails, I did scare up a deer however. After a while you can start
looking for a cement marker close to the ground on your right (there are a couple
before this on the left). X marks the spot (almost) from this marker count off
20 steps and find a path on your left. 15 steps down this path leads you to a
small, fallen tree and at it's base you'll find the Sweet William plant.
Stamp in, rehide carefully and return to the path. Just ahead is a pretty decent
overlook with some houses below but I'd suggest returning the way you came
unless you know the area. When I got back to the blue trail I took that straight
ahead and came out to the same hilltop but took a different path down.
