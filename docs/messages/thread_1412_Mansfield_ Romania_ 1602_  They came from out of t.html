Mansfield Center, Romania, 1602:

More than a hundred monsters spewed forth from the hellmouth that
opened it's maw under Mansfield last weekend. A classic, small,
quaint, New England area of hills and forests and fine old dwellings,
reduced to fear. Answering the call, hunters from all over,
descended upon the normally sleepy town and outnumbered the fiends
four or five to one. Night Owl and I were fortunate to accompany a
team from the south, of MD/VA hunters. We were led by a
relentless 'Van Helsing like' hunter of wide renown: Dead Duck
Chuck.

Arriving at dusk on Friday our team forebore any respite from the
long journey, swallowed their fear, and plunged into the forest.
Even the young pixie sprite, Claire de Lune, braved the shadows and
oncoming dark.

Such monsters to behold! Frankenstein by Team Green Dragon, wrapped
in black velvet and tied in red ribbon. Werewolves, phantoms,
vampires. Strange noises in the dark. Hoo Hoo,hoo Woo. "Stop
that! You're scaring me." "I didn't do it." Twice we were lost in
the dark in the unfamiliar woods, despairing of our quest. Finally,
just before the hour of nine, we found our last quarry near the
mysterious obelisk by the headless cemetery.

"Get what rest you can," advises our Van Helsing, Dead Duck
Chuck, "we hunt again at dawn." Night Owl burned candle into the
wee hours mapping out strategy, literally, upon a locally obtained
atlas. I carved a last minute impression to share with fellow
hunters at the gather (the Borg Queen, which I afterwards brought
homeward to start a new tale).

From dawn until into the rainy night, the next day we hunted.

In the afternoon we paused to exchange hunting art with others of
our kind, a mad frenzy of four or five hundred fellow hunters.
(Strangely we met only few of them on the trails.) In the
disorienting crowd I made only some fifty new exchanges, but that
without even much trying. Tempus Fugit had the right idea to bring
camp chairs, park in a quieter corner, and let people come to us.
The line to stamp in to the event log stretched long for a couple
hours. Food, music, bingo, trivia contests, raffles -- the hunters
knew how to gather. Psychologically disoriented, though
exhilirated, by crowds, I could barely acknowledge those I was
anxious to see, or to meet. More than once I searched through the
crowd for an impression I had seen in someone else's journal. In the
course of the afternoon, the other relentless hunters talked me out
of my more obscure travellers.

Off again, we hunted and hunted on into the dark and rainy night.
Our journey here, a 16 hour round trip, we needed to make the most
of it. Van Helsing/Dead Duck Chuck often plunged ahead to the next
hunt while we cleaned up the last. Faithful companion, Tuxie, often
had to lead us to him. So determined were we, so driving our Van
Helsing, that we left Wanda and Pete in our wake as we rushed
onwards. "Great to see you, gotta run." Through the gorgeous forest
scene we hunted: towering trees, babbling brooks, little waterfalls,
little wood bridges, leaves beginning to change, colorful fungus, a
river of rocks to climb, boulders, and stone walls, stone walls,
stone walls, stone walls.

We hunted Universal monsters, TV monsters, Munsters, classical
monsters, screaming monsters, monsters with bad teeth, with one eye,
too many legs, cartoons, cookie monsters, science fiction wonders,
and "The Attack of the 50 Foot Woman".

Late that night we found ourselves in a quaint old country store,
with a quaint proprietess, contemplating a treasure chest containing
a fabulously wrought Gollum.

In the end, after two nights, a day, and a morning, we had hunted
down some sixty of the fiends. We left a lot of impressions.

Scarab of the Doubtful Guests
