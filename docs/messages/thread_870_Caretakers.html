From: cpascott | Date: 2004-06-24 17:56:42 UTC
This message, with slightly different content, was posted on the
letterbox-usa group. I wanted to post parts of it here as the boxes
I'm hoping to place are all in the Northeast.


Hey all!

Although I'm new to this hobby and quite eager to get out there and
find more letterboxes, I'm itching to place a few as well. As I
alluded to on another thread, the challenge I see is the area near my
home is fairly saturated with letterboxes and as I try to think of
unique an interesting places to plant, many of my ideas are of places
that are at least a couple of hours from home.

The challenge with placing boxes outside of a certain radius is that
they become very hard to care for. Although I'm enthusiastic about
letterboxing, I'm not as willing to spend all of my free time
revisiting every place I put a letterbox to make sure it's still in
great condition and hasn't gone missing.

From what I've read around here it seems that people vary in how much
and how often they care for their boxes. Some are quite dilligent
about it, others more lax. Some have placed boxes only near their
hoome, others have placed boxes while on vacation -- knowing full
well that they cannot maintain them and may never see them again.

[CONTENT REMOVED -- see letterbox-usa]

My next point concerns adoption of boxes. For those boxes that I'm
hoping to plant that are outside of an area I can reasonably
maintain, I was thinking that I could plant and then effectively
orphan the box immediately. As long as people reported the box
as "alive and well", I'd have no worries. Because 'boxers routinely
carry extra zip-log bags, etc. I'd be reasonably comfortable that the
integrity of the box could be maintained. An issue would only arise
if some of the boxes contents (notably the stamp and logbook) were
damaged or missing, if the logbook was full, or if the box itself
disappeared.

To handle these situations I thought I might recruit a caretaker.
Not necessarily the same as an "adopter", the boxes would still be
mine I would bear the trouble of maintaining them. The caretaker
would be a person who, when requested, would visit the box to confirm
a report of damaged contents, missing box, etc. I would then work
with the caretaker to correct the problem or decide to retire the
box. If the opportunity arose to visit the box again, I would.

Box reports would still filter to me. If the box's logbook was full,
for example, a finder would report that to me, I'd get in touch with
the caretaker and ask them to replace the logbook with another and
ship the full logbook to me. I'd then pay for the cost of the new
logbook and shipping of the old one.

As I place letterboxes outside my home area I'd develop essentially a
team of caretakers helping me maintain my placed boxes that are too
difficult for me to keep up on my own.

As for hooking up caretakers with my boxes, I'd request anyone
willing to help out to first find the box using only my clues. They
could then count the box as a find and would know its exact location
if it needed maintenance. If they were willing to visit the box from
time to time just to check on it (like adoption) that would be great,
but not necessary.

Does this idea sound plausible? I won't yet divulge the areas I'm
planning on planting boxes. I was thinking I would recruit a
caretaker(s) as I prepare to plant a box outside my home area or
shortly after the box has been planted. I have a list of potential
boxes I'm hoping to create ... 5 individual boxes and 1 6-box
series. Only 3 of these 11 boxes would be in my home area, and even
one of those might be difficult to consistently maintain. I also
have an idea for a large 11-box driving tour series that I'd place in
an area outside my home region.

In summary:
1. [ONLY POSTED ON LETTERBOX-USA]
2. Do you think my "caretaker" idea is a good one?

ADDITIONAL COMMENTS SPECIFIC TO LbNE:
I should mention that from an earlier thread a couple of people have
already mentioned they would adopt boxes I placed close to them, but
as my message above speaks to, I'm trying to either modify what
adoption means or develop an entirely new concept called "caretaking".

Thoughts are appreciated!

CPAScott

welcome

I am happy to hear of your interest in boxing. I'm also very happy to hear that somebody is
forward thinking about the population of boxes in proximity to other boxes. That brings
up an important point that we should consider. In sensitive wildlife spots and trails, there
should be some consideration given to the number of boxes in a given area. Though I
love connecticut for it's abundance of boxes, i also treasure the state of Maine and the fact
that the boxes are few and far between. This makes them special and unique and yes,
hard to come by.

Thank you for bringing this thought to the talk list. I hope that with informed people who
are also thoughtful of the natural surroundings we also have good letterbox placement.
sometimes I think people are afraid to say that somebody might have a box in a really bad
area. I think i was called on this more than once, thank you to Giddy. I really didn't think
carefully about the Saco Heath and lo and behold, it needed to be moved ....and for the
better!

As a "placer" I really appreciate feedback like this from people who are out there finding
boxes. If you think that somebody has a box in an area that is way far off the trail or even
dangerous, you should by all means call them on it. Thank you for bringing this up!

phyto (maine)

"Adoption" can mean as much or as little as you want, and be decided by you
and whoever offers to help on a box-by-box basis. I've adopted boxes
planted in the Finger Lakes after the original owner moved out of state,
but the boxes remain in his name, the logbooks still carry his email
address and box reports still go directly to him. I just check up on his
boxes every now and then to make sure they're in good shape. I send him a
little update whenever I check in on them, he lets me know if they need
special attention based on a finder's report, and that works well for us.

I've also adopted local boxes by people who have moved away, were never
local in the first place or have lost interest in LBing altogether. These
boxes are listed under my name on the LBNA site, the clues link to my own
page, box reports come to me but I still list the original owners as
creators and haven't changed one word of their clues. I *am* itching to
change one word in one box's clues since the way it's written now leads to
lost of needless (IMO) time searching in the wrong area. But the planter
already knew it led to confusion and didn't want to adjust the clue, so I
respect her wishes even though she's no longer maintaining the box.

Just let people know up front how you want to handle
adoption/caretaking/whatever you choose to call it. I can't imagine that
anyone who's willing to help in the first place would have any problem with
your preferences.

From: trishkri | Date: 2004-06-24 19:29:56 UTC
This also brings up the related topic of "how long should a box
remain in place?"

I am in the process of retiring most of my boxes for several reasons.

- I don't have time to maintain them properly

- I don't feel that a box should remain in one spot too long as it
can be very hard on the local environment (this is in keeping with
the Dartmoor philosophy of boxing).

- I was lucky enough to get into boxing when RI only had a few boxes
so I was able to plant *anywhere* I wanted without overpopulating an
area. It is hubris on my part to feel that my boxes are more
important than newcomers who may be interested in planting boxes of
their own but feel like all of the *good spots* are taken (RI is
only so big...)

BUT, I do have few boxes that are my favorite that I maintain and
plan on keeping in place (albeit a slightly relocated place to avoid
damage to the area).

My 2c,
Trish
