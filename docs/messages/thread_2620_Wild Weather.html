Wild Weather

From: Mary Ellen Martel | Date: 2005-12-11 16:43:15 UTC
Happy Holidays all!

Just a request that when people are sharing information such as weather, hiking conditions, etc., it is helpful to include where you are from. Even though I've been involved with letterboxing for a while now, I don't have a good sense in my head of where everyone is from. The weather reports are interesting, but would be even more so if I had a clue what the locations were!

For those who might be interested, Mount Desert Island and Acadia National Park received between 8 and 10 inches by the looks. Wet snow that stuck to everything and made for a gorgeous commute yesterday morning. I, for one, will have my fingers crossed for a wonderfully snowy winter here on the island so that I can take lots of beautiful photographs! While the snow may make stealhy letterboxing difficult, we can always scout out potential new locations while enjoying the beauty of winter. I think I like winter hiking more than summer. (Which may be due in part to the lack of tourists here on the island now!)

~ Memlili

What is Salem Series

From: Team Randalstik | Date: 2006-01-20 01:47:48 UTC
I regret to inform all that the "What is Salem" series in Salem, MA, is
being pulled temporarily due to an unsafe plant on the first box and
probably the second. I did not know this when I planted it, but the small
park where this first box is planted is a known "hang out for weirdos"
according to the police who responded to the 911 distress call from a
letterboxer.

I apologize for the poorly placed box and hope no one else has had any
trouble if they have attempted these boxes.

The boxes will be replanted in a safer location in the future.

In the meantime, box smart, carry a cell phone, and box with a partner.

Team Randalstik

trekking poles & containers

From: Silent Doug | Date: 2006-01-23 15:04:27 UTC
I saw that Ocean State Job Lot in Canton has collapsible trekking
poles on sale for just $6.88 each. This is a great price for a
starter trekking pole, so if you've ever wanted to try them, this is
a good chance! FYI, every time you plant a trekking pole as you walk,
you transfer stress off of your knees and legs to your arms and
shoulders. As a result, you get less stress on knee joints, and you
get a bit of an upper body workout! I've read that using trekking
poles when going downhill can result in up to 40% less strain on your
knees, and I really have noticed a difference on my own weak knees at
the end of a hard day of hiking since I started using trekking poles.
I've actually been using the exact same pole as the ones sold at
Ocean State -- they're cheap, but that way I won't feel bad if I lose
one (again) or break one (again)... <g>.

I also saw that they have "Freshness Keepers" -- a "Lock 'n' Load"
type container. The 30 oz version is just $1.29. I picked one up to
test it out, but it seems promising at a good price.

I imagine that the other Ocean State stores in CT and RI may have
these, as well.

SD

I generally use mine by planting a pole only once per pace (er,
that's 2 steps <g>), instead of with every footfall. If I'm climbing
or descending a hill, I'll plant a pole with each footfall of the opposite leg.

Another tip -- the strap on the handle isn't supposed to go around
your wrist. The strap is supposed to go around the palm of your hand
(between your thumb and index finger), and then be reasonably tight
against your hand. Then you grasp the handle with the strap in
between your hand and the handle. Now you don't have to clinch your
hand so tightly to hold onto the pole. (In Nordic walking, your hand
is supposed to open completely off the grip when you bring it
forward, but that's way too complicated for me!)
