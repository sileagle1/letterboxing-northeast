Re: Yet another letterboxing article

From: Memlili | Date: 2006-12-17 04:14:49 UTC
I hesitate to share anything about this article, as I have been the
target of wrath on this board before, but as someone who was
interviewed for the article, I feel I should set a couple of things
straight.

#1 ~ The clues quoted in the story belong to a latterbox placed by
one of the people interviewed for the article ~ a personal friend of
mine. She is one of the people pictured in the photograph. I
suspect that, especially in New England, there are lots of
letterboxes in wooded areas with stone pillars and stone walls, but
in this case the reporter had found the box, contacted the placer
about the interview and went back to the site with the placer to
take the photo for the article.

#2 ~ The reporter made a point to stress the importance of being
secretive and rehiding well. He also directed people to the web
sites and to the advice for beginners.

#3 ~ The reporter had done some letterboxing himself and wanted to
share this new-found hobby with others.

#4 ~ I thought he was actually rather clever in showing photos that
were totally unrelated to illustrate the article. Again, I know the
people shown opening the letterbox. and the location, as well as the
directions, have nothing whatsoever to do with the Penobscot Narrows
box.

#5 ~ The reporter was going to write the article whether anyone
talked with him or not, because he was excited about letterboxing.
At least 3 out of the 4 people interviewed talked at length with him
about the importance of NOT divulging any identifying information
because of the threat of vandalism to boxes.

If this article upset people, I fear there isn't an article that can
be written that won't offend. The reality is that letterboxing is
out there in the public eye. If a reporter discovers the hobby and
decides to write about it, I would like to think that by talking
with him and really stressing the importance of not giving away
locations, the article may have been less harmful to the hobby than
if he had been left to his own ideas with no input from people who
care about the hobby.

I fully expect to be ripped apart on this message board as I have
been in the past, but I felt a need to give another side to the
story.

Memlili
aka Mary Ellen Martel

From: Lisa (Taplin) Murray | Date: 2006-12-17 18:22:21 UTC
ME- Thanks for writing this. I actually thought that the reporter did
a pretty good job, honestly. I figured he had permission to take the
pictures (or hoped he did) I do know that the Peacock's Pandora is
pretty much left there (or at least I never got any response when I
emailed them several years ago)

Sangfroid really thought about whether to contribute or not. On one
hand, he didn't want to be part of an article "exposing" letterboxing
(esp. due to the nature of placing boxes around MDI) and on the other
he hoped he could add an angle to an article so that people would
understand that the "hobby" has many dimensions and people need to be
responsible. In the end, he decided that his boxing style is to go
"under the radar" so he felt speaking for an interview would not be in
his best interest.

I was THRILLED that they interviewed Memlilli! Her boxes are the first
I found and the series mentioned in the article is fantastic.

My concern is just general publicity, but I plan on doing more mystery
and WOM anyway, and I was also concerned by the mention that you can
hike in ACadia and find 4 or 5 boxes right off the trail (which isn't
cool because boxes aren't allowed in Acadia at all)

Mainly, I wanted to just let you all know about the article because
there are so many great boxes up here and this is the first article
about boxing in the area.

Re: Yet another letterboxing article

From: beswick_04 | Date: 2006-12-19 10:47:01 UTC
The Penobscot Narrows Letterbox belongs to us (Beswick) and
permission was NOT granted to photograph it and we would have
preffered that it had not. It is considered bad form to show the
contents of a letterbox of someone who was not interviewed and should
have shown one of those who were. BUT, this is the chance we took,
letterboxes are set "free" and become public property where by right
they can be photographed.

The only thing that really disappoints us with the photograph in the
article is that the surprise of seeing the stamp has been taken away
for those that hunt for the box.

We would like to acknowledge that the article was very well written,
one of the better that we have seen. We found it to be responsible
and informative, thereby minimizing the negative impact to the hobby
and increasing the positive impact.

Well, in the spirit of the Holidays we'll take it as a compliment that
they picked our stamp to show.

Beswick

care in these articles to protect the boxes that are already there.
