From: Mary Ellen Martel | Date: 2006-06-25 15:34:40 UTC
At risk of offending people, I feel I must respond to this post. When loons are incubating their eggs the future chicks are very vulnerable to being abandoned by the parents if they are disturbed by humans. The loon in the photos defininately looks agitated. In 2004 the state of New Hampshire had just over 500 adult loons. Just over 100 chicks are born in a season. These numbers make every single egg incredibly precious. When humans encroach upon a nesting loon, we are putting the potential offspring in danger. Loons in northern states such as New Hampshire and Maine are incubating eggs right now and chicks will be hatching over the next few weeks. In the spirit of letterboxing's creed of "leave no trace", let's all be aware of these threatened and endangered birds and give them a wide berth if we happen upon a nest or family. If you would like to learn more about loons and watch a nesting pair, go to: http://www.briloon.org/ed/looncam.htm

Memlili

From: Team Randalstik | Date: 2006-06-25 18:17:55 UTC
I can concur with Skier444. The trail is far enough away that it
would be difficult to get that shot without a zoom. GREAT SHOT!

I saw the same loon when doing that series too. I was shocked when i
saw a loon and even more shocked when I stopped and it began swimming
towards me. I grew up in Minnesota where the Loon is the state bird
and the closest I have EVER been to a LIVE loon is about 100 yards.
This loon swam UP TO ME! I thought, "my, NH loons sure are friendly."
But be rest assured I did nothing to approach the loon nor try to get
it closer (my first thought was, "do Loons get Rabies? Is this just a
crazy loon?"). Then my friend saw that the loon was crawling onto its
nest. So then we ran away, afraid that we may be on some reality TV
show, "When Loons go bad."

The nest was about 10 yards away from the trail, I thought that is
one risky place. We'll see how it does.

Glad all are so understanding of the delicacy of the loon.

The other incredible thing was the plethora of Pink Lady Slippers in
bloom! Another endangered species I believe. The Pink Lady Slipper
Orchid is the Minnesota State Flower and I have NEVER seen one
before. Well, they were all over the place at this park. Those flower
lovers will have to go in Mid June next year to see the flower in
bloom. Amazing!

Team Randalstik
