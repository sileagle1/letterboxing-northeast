From: StarSaels | Date: 2006-08-13 02:23:02 UTC
MK:

You could try what I do with the blue-rimmed Rubbermaid letterboxes
I use: first I use a Sharpie to write "This is a Letterbox. This is
NOT a Geocache! Remove NOTHING from this box!" on the outside clear
part of the lid. Then I trace what I wrote (now backwards) on the
INSIDE of the lid, so that even if the outside wears off the inside
is still there.

As I make my own logbooks and usually design my covers on the
computer, I include a similar, though longer message on the cover of
the logbook.

You could also use some kind of tether to attach the stamp and
logbook to the inside of the letterbox, although that would negate
the waterproofing qualities of the ziploc bags. ;-)

In the event things still go awry, you could post a message on the
geocaching boards, asking the knuckleheads to put the stamp back in
your letterbox.

StarSAELS

This phenomenon seems to be related to the following instances.

1) Placement overlap

By this I mean that either the cache was first or the box first, or
they both overlap and neither the two shall ever meet unless the minds
think alike. I believe I posted to this list a short while ago my
experience when placing a box in the Wiscasset area. I had thought
long and hard about the Nickels-Sortwell sunken garden spot. Lo and
behold, the day that I go to install my box, the EXACT spot that I
decided upon had a geocache already there! I was astounded for many
reasons primarily because I made the assumption that cachers do not
always choose good spots, but in this case it was the perfect location
and I thought it unwise to plant a box and overlap the same spot.

2) Online clues

Both letterboxing and geocaching rely (exclusively in the case of
geocaching) on a web-based online format for clue distribution and
logging. I'm not saying this is bad, but it makes the clues easy to
get for boxers and cachers alike. So for instance, if someone on the
geocaching site decided that they didn't want a particular letterbox
in an area, then who is to say that someone couldn't go look up your
LBNA clue listing and remove the box? I'm not saying that a
letterboxer or cacher would do this, it's just an example of how
easily the clues can be obtained.

Now, does either hobby promote the other? Well, not really, though I
believe the LBNA links listing might put geocaching there as does
Jeremy's geocaching site link to LBNA, but does that mean that the
participants go to the trouble to look up either clue areas? Probably
not. I would guess that there are many geocaches in the area that we
currently reside judging from reports from fellow boxers and just
knowing the type of people that live in this part of Maine, but there
are hardly any boxes. I'm not about to go and look up all the
geocaches, but I will take that into consideration if it seems as
though certain areas might already be populated.

Finally...

3) Growing hobby

With the advent of the internet-based clue distribution as well as
media attention and magazine articles in everything from travel guides
to whatnot, there's no saying how many new players come into the fold
each year. Now if you think about the surrounding area you live and
the radius of clues, it can only get smaller and smaller as more and
more people enter the hobby and start placing.

To answer your question, is there any way to let cachers know that our
hobby has different rules? Well, the steadfast rule of boxing is that
THERE ARE NO RULES... so if we as a community cannot even come to a
consensus about that rule first and foremost, there is no way that we
can expect the greater geocaching community to just "get it"... no?

And finally, it's up to them and YOU as a responsible boxer to just
educate yourself about each hobby as much as you can with what
information is available, but don't expect that just because the rules
are out there that folks will actually read them or adhere to any
policies. The best insurance in my experience is to limit the access
to clues and make better judgements about box placement. There is no
need for a rash of new, poorly placed boxes in an area that is already
over-populated with caches and other boxes. Just my opinions on the
matter. I can't see this getting any better especially in southern maine.

phyto

A Good way to avoid confusion is to write, in permanent marker, on the
outside of the box "LETTERBOX" and maybe even "NOT A GEOCACHE", and
then ALSO, with a different colored marker, mirror that writing on the
INSIDE of the box, directly behind the outside lettering.

I use Red or bright blue on the outside of the box, and Black on the
inside of the box. This way if the outside writing wears off, the
inside writing is still there. It's worth it in the long run.

YahYah
