From: moonstone_baby@yahoo.com | Date: 2003-12-17 00:14:29 UTC
Hi Kathryn,

I feel fortunate that I live in Rhode Island where there are so many
letterboxes. I was down in New Jersey over Columbus day weekend and
did some nice boxes in New Jersey and near by Pennsylvania (Delaware
Water Gap.) Silent Doug plants boxes in PA.

Anyway, here are some of my ideas:

1. Get your local newspaper to do an article on letterboxing. Both
the Providence Journal in RI and the Hartford Courant in CT have done
nice articles on letterboxing. Get a journalist who will go out on
the trail with you and take pictures and really play it up.

2. Organize a gathering in your area and put the word out
publically.

3. Is there and EMS store or equivalent outdoors store in your
area? Try to get them involved (this is also a good bulletin board
place for postings)

4. Are there any land trusts or other organizations in your area
with newsletters? That's how I first heard about letterboxing over 2
years ago. Someone had planted boxes in a land trust and the land
trust newsletter did an article on the boxes. My friend read the
article and told me we should go do letterboxing. Then I heard that
EMS had clues for letterboxes at their stores in CT. Also, the
Federal Wildlife Refuge across the street from my house has the clues
to their letterbox right out in plain site at their vistors center.
Lots of people walk through there and see the clues and ask about it.
(PS the box is padlocked to prevent vandalism and you must do
the "quest" to figure out the combination to the letterbox lock.)

I hope this helps.

Moonstone_baby

Yes, I do. I first started letterboxing at my home in RI in 2001.
When I returned to my home in Central MA, there was only *one*
letterbox in Central MA - Bonsai. I quickly started to create and
plant boxes along the 495 route. If you plant them, they will come.

Look at Central MA now!

In my experience, if you plant a whole bunch of boxes in an area, and
folks who are just itching to see new letterboxes come to play hide
and seek, they are often enthralled by the area and come back to
plant their own.

But your mileage may vary.

Greetings,

I very strongly believe in the first law of
letterboxing. "Plant, and they will come!"

When we got involved in this hobby two years ago,
there were about 70 boxes in Western MA. Since then,
we've planted about 40 more, with the hoped for
results. The more boxes there are in an area, the more
likely it is that new people will stick around long
enough to plant a box or three.

For those of you who have been wondering what the heck she is talking
about, we do a program series called "Lunch and Learns" at the Wells
Reserve where volunteers, staff, and community members make an
informal presentation on a topic of interest that is at least vaguely
related to nature and the environment. People bring a bag lunch and we
provide them with free dessert and drinks if they bring a reuseable mug.
I decided that I would do one on letterboxing to try and get some more
people interested in it and to promote the boxes that we have on site
and in southern Maine. I've included the description below (notice the
similarity to the description on the LBNA site?)in case anyone is
interested in helping or you know someone who might be interested in
learning more about letterboxing. I am fairly new to the hobby myself,
so if folks have suggestions or images or clues they think I should
mention or feature, feel free to drop me a line.
Nautilus

Lunch and Learn: Letterboxing
Wed, Apr 28, noon to 1 pm
Letterboxing is an intriguing outdoor hobby that combines treasure
hunting, orienteering, hiking, and art. Interested individuals hide a
box containing a logbook and a carved stamp in a beautiful,
interesting, or sometimes remote location, then compose clues that are
posted to a website. Letterboxing has something for everyone; people
of all ages and backgrounds participate. Come learn the history of
this unique pastime, discover how to become involved, and view some
stunning carved images collected on the trail. Laura Lubelczyk, Wells
Reserve education director and letterboxing enthusiast, presents this
program. Bring and bag lunch and a mug for free drinks.
Reservations are requested for this free program. Please call (207)
646-1555.

As this description will appear in our calendar, it doesn't include
information on where it will be held, so here it is:
This program will be held in the Mather Auditorium at the Wells
National Estuarine Research Reserve at Laudholm Farm located in Wells,
Maine. For directions, visit www.wellsreserve.org.

That's a good point! But I'd also like to point out that the more
boxes there are in an area, the more people will stummble into it by
accident. I can't begin to count the number of people that have told

for a letterbox while doing a Google search for some trail or place
where I hid a letterbox.

One more suggestion that just came to mind. As explaining letterboxing
as a hobby can be rather involved - take extra copies of the
letterboxing fact sheet. Or make your own based on this idea.

It can be found here:
http://members.aol.com/Letterboxr/infosheet.html

It has been my experience that when presenting this type of activity
to a group (especially children) that it needs to be described in a
concise manner so that all ages understand. If you're interested in
designing a quest you can find all kinds of information on questing
from the Valley Quest web page:
http://www.vitalcommunities.org/ValleyQuest/ValleyQuest.htm

Best of luck to you!
