> What is your feeling about placing some sort of "time needed" on

In my own experience I have stayed away from putting a time needed on
the clue for a number of personal reasons.

First of all because everyone's own style of boxing varies. Someone
with children might take 3 times as long. As I don't have children, I
can't be a good judge of that time needed. As someone who has taught
children and led countless nature hikes, I can guess that it would
take longer, but out of fairness - I don't put that time because I
honestly don't know.

Secondly, a casual hiker versus someone who is on a boxing mission are
two different things. Usually I hike to view nature, and that takes
time. When you're in the city however and doing urban boxes - it can
be understood that you've gotta be slick and stamping in should be
done quickly and the clue should be somewhat straightforward.

Finally, if someone can't figure out from the clue or a personal
contact or research ahead of time how long it's going to take, then
they can always opt not to find that box or come back to it another
time when they can spend as much time as they want or is needed.

> Does anyone out there feel it takes something
> away from the hunt?

No - but it's just like reading on a database:"easy drive by" when
it's not in the clue at all...it sort of gives it away.

In general I don't think about the time it takes to get a box. If I
start early enough it's not a problem, I'm not usually ever pressed
for time unless it's already dusk when I start, and then it's my own
fault for not bringing a flashlight or headlamp (which I usually bring)


> Do you ever decide which boxes to do or not do..
> based upon time needed?

No - but I/we always thoroughly read the clues and/or research the
area and trail before heading out so that we feel somewhat prepared.
In New England I'm fairly familiar with most areas, so I feel as
though I have a good handle on how long it would take.


> What is your decision if there is no
> indication of distance or time needed?

Personally, it does not make a difference if the time is noted.

From: rscarpen | Date: 2004-01-06 01:36:08 UTC
> What is your feeling about placing some sort of "time needed" on
> letterboxing clues?

You know the saying--"It's the thought that counts"? =) I like the
concept, but the implementation generally isn't very good because
there can be such a wide variation between hiking speeds among
letterboxers. I'd much rather have concrete distances and elevation
gains mentioned in the clue so I can figure out how long it should
take me to do.

I also tend to add a one-word summary of the terrain difficulty in
my clues (easy, medium, hard, strenuous, whatever). It's more of a
subjective measure, but I also include a link where people can get a
rough idea of what I think is "easy", "medium", etc. I'm thinking
about eliminating it, though, since it doesn't seem to add anything
that anyone thinks is useful. And I get plenty of people that
say, "But sheeze! Your EASY hikes take me all freaking day!!!!"
But with the trail's length and elevation gain mentioned right next
to it, it's pretty stupid of me to throw out my subjective opinions
about the trail difficulty.

Not that it hasn't stopped me.... =)

-- Ryan

From: Steve and Heidi | Date: 2004-01-06 05:25:53 UTC
We try to include a general idea of how long the hike should take.
Obviously, I can make it through a hike like Avery Tract (3 hours) in
way under that time, but it is because I am familiar with the land.
I've had boxes listed as "1/2 hour" take us 2 hours because the clues
were vague. Its just a general guideline and should be taken at face
value.

As far as segregating hikes based upon time, why would you NOT do
that?? I work a lot of night shifts and a lot of my hiking is done
in the afternoon where you may have 2 or 3 hours daylight left.
Saving shorter hikes for those days works well. When I have a full
day available, I do longer hikes or multiple boxes in an area.

Perhaps if you do not want to commit to a time frame on your clues
you can say something more like "3 miles strenuous hiking with steep
elevation" versus "1/2 mile round trip, flat terrain". Thats a big
help and I know how quick I can get a rough mile done on different
terrains.

Hi Kstar. In my clues, I generally give the time it takes me to do
the hike or else the distance, if I know it. I am a slow
walker/hiker, so in general other people do hikes in less time
than I do. By the same token, I appreciate being given the
approx. distance or time in a clue when I hunt for boxes. I usually
figure it will take me a bit longer 'cause I am not a speedy hiker.

I sometimes try to work out a plan to do several boxes in one
day. Knowing the estimated time it takes to find a box helps me
decide which boxes to do that day and in what order.

However, I have learned to be flexible and not get my heart set on
anything going according to plan. The more flexible and open to
the unexpected I am, the more I enjoy letterboxing. A couple of
weeks ago I went on a hike that took about twice as long as I
anticipated and I never did find the box, but I didn't mind at all, as
it was a beautiful place and an exhilarating winter walk. I'll try
there again in dry weather when the landmarks are not hidden
under snow!

Thanks for initiating this discussion. It's very informative to learn
how others feel about this aspect of clue writing.

From: rscarpen | Date: 2004-01-06 20:42:30 UTC
> when walking, how do you know how far you've walked?

Most of the time, I try to find an official source that shows the
distance. A trail map, guide book, or on the Internet. If all else
fails, I'm pretty good at estimating the distance based on how long
it takes me to walk. While everyone tends to hike at different
speeds, the same person tends to walk at the same pace whereever
they go. With rugged or steep terrain, I may move at two miles per
hour. With flat, easy terrain, I may fly by at three miles per
hour. So if you know how long you've been walking, you can estimate
how far you've walked.

Pedometers are kind of fun to play with too. They count how many
steps you've taken, so if you know your stride (easily measurable),
they'll give a pretty good estimate of the actual distance
traveled. Keep in mind that even with a pedometer, different
terrain require different strides, even for the same person.

But like I said before, an official source for trail lengths is
usually easy to find on a map, book, or Internet. But pedometers
are FUN! =)

-- Ryan

From: Janet | Date: 2004-01-07 01:01:34 UTC
I agree with Rustypuff. I like to see distances rather than time but
in lieu of distance time is good. Descriptions are very helpful
too. True that everyone hikes at different rates, but I use a time
listed in a clue as a guideline, not an absolute. If the clue says 1
hour to complete I wouldn't expect it to take me 5 hours but I would
not be surprised if it took up to 2 hours.

Do I use time/distance listed when selecting a letterbox?
Absolutely. I start early in the morning and go all day. I try to
plan my most demanding hikes early in the day and the easier hikes in
the afternoon. I don't plan on being out after dark (especially in
January.)

Safety is another consideration. I recently had someone say to me
that a letterbox hike they did was much longer than they thought it
would be from reading the clues. (no distance or time was given)
They had two children with them and would have picked a different
hike if they had realized how long it was. There are a lot of people
letterboxing. Not all these people have the level of "woodsmanship"
that you would expect from a typical hiker. They may over estimate
their abilites or underestimate the difficulty of a hike. Or they
just might make a mistake. Giving them guidance is at least helpful.

Everyone has a different approach to writing their letterbox clues.
I think that is one of the beauties of the sport, how much creativity
and individualism there is. In general, my letterboxes are not
difficult and provide the letterboxer with very unambiguous
information. That is my style. Others enjoy making their boxes
mysterious and more ambiguous .... that is part of the fun of doing
their boxes (and I don't just mean mystery boxes.) So there is no
right or wrong answer to your question, just different opinions and
preferences.

that is my very long winded opinion.
