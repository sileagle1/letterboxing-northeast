Franklin. Ct. letterboxing day

From: rospa10 | Date: 2004-07-20 01:01:20 UTC
Today I found all of the 5 letterboxes placed in Franklin. A rain
shower had just passed through as I parked at Bailey's Ravine. Thus,
the forest was all drippy-wet, bugs were relatively nill and air was
a tad cooler. Note: here and at other l.b. site the rocks on trail
are very slippery when wet. Take care.
Mention in one logbook of bears created one of those "aha!" moments
regarding the trees which had been obviously used as gigantic
scratching posts. I plan to attach bells to my backpack on future
hikes to more remote woods as a warning to critters of the larger
sort of my approach. Generally, I'm a quiet hiker who treads lightly
in hopes of observing birds.
I also tracked down to the Tell Tale Apples l.b. by Irish Tinker.
I.T. is famous for retelling historic stories in an informative yet
entertaining manner. (Lovely spot but tricky clues.) This box had
moisture inside. I doublebagged the contents. Edge of box has a
crack which is probably letting in water.
If you haven't yet, treat yourself to an afternoon of letterboxing
in Franklin. Clue sheets provide accurate info. re. the hikes
involved.
Catlover/teacher/gardener

Catlover/teacher/gardener sez:
<<I doublebagged the contents.>>

and we say "yeah!" I think it was CSCM who once pointed out that it
is the double ziplocks and not the rubbermaid that is most important
for keeping logbooks dry. We always double bag our own and really
appreciate it when others follow suit. If there are only two ziplocks
in a box, they're both for the log...the rubber stamp doesn't need
its own.

We've been doing our summer rounds refreshing our letterboxes and
have also been surprised at the number that are poorly rehidden. From
the logbooks you can be pretty certain who it was that didn't care
enough to care enough. Sometimes we're a little surprised and hurt to
see familiar sig stamps as the last finders of boxes that are left
out with big "come kill me" signs to attract the Sabs.
