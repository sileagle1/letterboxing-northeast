Spring has sprung, and with it comes box maintenance.

I designated this week as my MA letterbox maintenance week.

It hasn't been a good week.

So far, 8 of my active MA letterboxes are almost entirely destroyed.
Could the common element be a particularly savage winter? I so wish
that were the case. The common element in the 8 destroyed letterboxes
was the last finders. In all 8 cases, the last finders did not:

1. Repack the logbook into its ziplock
2. Repack the stamp and inkpad into its ziplock
3. Put the lid back on the letterbox container
4. Rehide the letterbox

Yes, folks, that's right. I am now the proud parent of 8 active
letterboxes whose logbooks are soaked through from snow/rainfall,
obliterating all the signature stamps from all the previous happy
finders - but there's a bright side! At least the now empty log pages
are brightly tinted the color of the inkpads since the last finders put

the inkpads back into the letterbox without putting the *cover* back on

the inkpads!

At least the savvy last finders used pigment ink for their signature
stamps - these images are there clear as day. And I should be grateful

I suppose that when these last finders were done stamping in, they
helpfully left the unsealed letterboxes quite conveniently out in the
open, on *top* of the rock/log/cairn under which the boxes were
supposed to be hidden. Of course, these nice clear images from the
last finder had only the notation of the date. No trail name.
Nothing. I can't even contact these last finders to offer to give them

well-intentioned (and I do mean that sincerely) instructions on not
only finding a box, but REHIDING a box.

Grrrr.....

I have repaired all of them with new logs and ink pads and ziplocks and

have rehidden them properly. So now all Warrior Woman boxes in MA
(with the exception noted below) are back in action, ready to go.

And now it gets worse.

Went out to the Ashland Reservoir today to do a spring checkup on my
Valentine Series. All three are gone. Well, sorta gone. I did find
some forensic evidence of their previous existence. In the general
vicinity of one box, I found the letterbox's rubbermaid container which

had been conveniently turned into a portapotty by some enterpreneurial
partygoer. Farther along the trail, I discovered that the logbooks had

been used for kindling for a keg party fire. All that was left was the

wire binding and some charred bits of logbook still clinging to them.
The last and final box, Thrill Me, I had replaced once already due to
vandalism. Fortunately, I had replaced it as a film canister microbox
which miraculously escaped notice.

After heaving a heavy sigh, I pocketed the "Thrill Me" microbox and
decided to turn it into a hitchhiker. When I got home and unrolled the

scroll log, I nearly screamed. Guess who was the last finder of this
series? Yep - that happy crew with the pigment ink who had left all my

other boxes open and exposed.

Rant over.

I am officially retiring the Valentine Series in Ashland. And I'd
advise anyone looking to plant around there to be very VERY careful
about where to hide the box. Oh, but wait! I guess it really doesn't
matter how well you hide the box if the last finder of your new box is
part of my happy little untraceable pigment ink crew.

Sorry. The rant is REALLY over now.

As soon as I can find the heart to make another scroll log, I'll
release "Thrill Me" as a hitchhiker - after all, aren't you *thrilled*
when you find a hitchhiker in a box?!!

Warrior Woman
pulling herself up by her bootstraps
