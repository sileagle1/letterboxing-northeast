After the event is over....

From: rscarpen | Date: 2004-01-24 16:45:33 UTC
I always assumed that event stamps, after their brief glow in the
sun, retired to a better place--wherever that might be. The event
stamp was one way to encourage people to go to the event. It was a
way to show that, "Yes, I made it to the Such-And-Such event." Event
stamps were special for that reason. They HAD to be hand-carved.
(Unlike hiding a store-bought stamp--which I don't particularly like,
but I'll at least put up with--but ooooh.... if someone dares to
bring a store bought event stamp, they'll never hear the end of it
from me and I'd refuse to count it as an event stamp.) So they had
to be hand-carved. They had to be unique. And they had to be
retired after the end of the event.

And just because an event stamp is 'out of play' doesn't mean someone
not at the event won't be able to enjoy it. They'll still be able to
see the stamped image in other people's logbooks. That's what
logbooks are for, right?! Why can't we admire a stamp without having
to claim it in our logbook?

There's an event stamp I carved for an event in the Pacific
Northwest. It's long since been retired; never used since the day of
the event. But LOTS of people still see the stamp, because I turned
it into a clue for a box I hid at the event. The stamp is a 'teasure
map' of the park where the event was hid, and X marks the spot where
the letterbox was hid. I swore the event stamp would never be used
after the day of the event, but I scanned the image in, put it on my
website, and anyone that wants to hunt down the letterbox can print
out an image of the stamp (i.e. clue) to take with them. If they
want to paste the imagine into their logbook, that's fine with me.
But as long as it's not a stamped image, only the few that made the
effort to go to the event can have an original impression.

If one were to hide an event stamp after the event, at least hiding
it in the park where the event occurred might mollify me somewhat,
but it still seems out of the letterboxing spirit.

Anyhow, just my two cents. For those that think retiring a stamp is
a shame because then nobody else can "enjoy" it, I'd just like to
remind them that that's simply not true. Logbooks will keep the
stamp in circulation for years and decades to come.

-- Ryan
