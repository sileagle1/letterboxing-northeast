Re: [LbNE] Re: Do you have boxes at Gillette Castle State Park??????????

From: donutz716 | Date: 2009-10-28 22:34:23 UTC
I had a nice conversation yesterday with the Supervisor of State Land in the Forestry department at Connecticut DEP.  He told me there is no policy and no permit required for planting letterboxes in State Forests in Connecticut.  Using common sense - boxes should not be placed in restricted areas, in stone walls in Glastonbury and Portland (rattlesnakes), or streams.  Any box that is in a dangerous place such as the edge of cliffs is not allowed as someone could get hurt.  If boxes are found in these areas, they will be removed, but the DEP isn't specifically looking at where letterboxes are being planted.  Boxes that are lost due to controlled burning or other foresting going on will be at the expense of the owner, not DEP. 

Gillette Castle is a State Park.  The supervisor of this park had issues with some boxes that were planted in a restricted and dangerous area, so he wanted to know where others boxes were in the park.  When I spoke with him, he didn't even ask me the name of the box, but gave me the OK for where I placed it as it was away from the area in question. 

Hopefully this clears up any questions.

Enjoy!
donutz716

RE: [LbNE] Re: Do you have boxes at Gillette Castle State Park??????????

From: Teresa Gallagher | Date: 2009-11-28 17:22:00 UTC
I think it's a fair issue on the part of park managers. I say that from the
perspective of a park manager and trail volunteer - the people and groups
that give and not just take are always given a higher priority and seen in a
better light. It's not just the State parks, but also local parks and
especially CFPA, who has an issue with geocaches and letterboxes on many of
their Blue-Blazed trails because much of the network crosses private
property and they do not have permission from the landowner for this
activity (this is a bigger problem with geocaches that are off-trail). I
spoke with someone once (not from CFPA but very close to them) who said "we
always remove those things when we find them, they're not supposed to be
there."



I had an idea awhile ago that I've been mulling around. The Quinnipiac
Trail (the State's oldest hiking trail, c. 1929) is in various stages of
neglect and they have been discussing whether or not to abandon the first
several miles because of this. These first few miles are located within
Quinnipiac River State Park in North Haven, adjacent to (but on the other
side of the highway from) Sleeping Giant State Park. Basically, they cannot
keep up with heavy plant growth that obscures the trail during the summer
along the river, including poison ivy and Japanese Knotweed. I mentioned
this to Rob Butterworth from CFPA awhile back and he said they are always
looking for volunteers to help. CFPA has a system where they have Trail
Managers for various sections of the trails. Perhaps we could have a CT
Letterboxers group adopt the first section of trail and save it? That kills
several birds with one stone - pleasing the State Parks people, pleasing the
CFPA people, and helps save the most historical trail in the entire state.



I have a lot of trail maintenance experience, as well as experience
pulling/spraying poison ivy and other invasive species. Anyone interesting
in something like that? Here's my blog post from the trail
http://sheltontrails.blogspot.com/2009/09/first-ct-trail-mile-one.html.
